IMPACT OF RAINFALL VARIATION ON FOREST DIEBACK IN HORTON PLACE NATIONAL PARK SRI LANKA.

More Information

Introduction: Why Forests Matter

Forests play an essential role in maintaining global ecosystems. They act as carbon sinks, regulate the climate, and provide habitats for countless species. However, in recent decades, forest dieback—the process where trees begin to decline and eventually die—has been observed in various regions. In tropical ecosystems like Horton Plains, Sri Lanka, forest dieback is particularly concerning, as the region is home to rich biodiversity and serves as a critical water source for the country. This blog post explores my research, which investigates how climatic variables, specifically rainfall and temperature, are affecting forest dieback in Horton Plains. By studying 20 years' worth of data, I aimed to uncover trends, assess the relationship between climate and dieback, and develop a model that can help predict future dieback events.

Research Goals

My research had two primary objectives:

Objective 1

Comprehensive Analysis of Temperature rainfall variation and forest dieback.

Objective 2

Development of Forest Dieback Prediction Model.

By achieving these goals, the study sought to provide valuable insights into the drivers of forest dieback in Horton Plains, which could help inform better forest management and conservation strategies.

Methods: Step-by-Step Process

(01) Gathering Data: Climate and Forest Health.

To understand the changes occurring in Horton Plains, I focused on two main types of data: monthly climate data and information on forest dieback.

  • Climate Data: I obtained monthly temperature and rainfall data for Horton Plains from the Department of Meteorology. This dataset spans from 2000 to 2020, providing valuable insights into climate trends over two decades.
  • Forest Dieback Data: Although direct forest dieback data was unavailable, I utilized satellite imagery. Through the Google Earth Engine (GEE) platform, I analyzed satellite images from Landsat 7 and Landsat 8 to assess forest health over the years. By calculating the Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI)—a standard measure for evaluating vegetation health—I estimated the extent of forest dieback each year.

(02) Drawing a Polygon in GEE for Horton Plains

To define the area of study, I first drew a polygon around Horton Plains in Google Earth Engine using geographical coordinates. This allowed me to isolate the satellite imagery for this specific region, ensuring that the analysis focused on the correct location.

(03) Calculating Forest Dieback Using GEE

Once the polygon was set, I used GEE to analyze annual satellite images and calculate the forest dieback area. By focusing on NDVI values, I could differentiate between healthy and degraded vegetation. Any area with an NDVI value below a certain threshold was classified as experiencing dieback. This method enabled me to estimate the forest dieback for each year from 2000 to 2020.

(04) Trend Analysis of Temperature, Rainfall, and Dieback

The next step was to look for patterns in the data. I performed a seasonal decomposition of the time series for temperature, rainfall, and dieback. This allowed me to break the data down into trend, seasonal, and residual components, helping to reveal the underlying long-term changes. Here’s what I found:

  • Temperature: A gradual rise in temperature over the years, consistent with broader climate change patterns.
  • Rainfall: Significant variability in rainfall from year to year, with no clear increasing or decreasing trend.
  • Forest Dieback: Fluctuations in dieback, with higher levels during drier years.

These trends provided valuable context for the next phase of the research: assessing how these climate variables relate to forest dieback.

(05) Quantifying the Relationship Between Climate and Dieback

To better understand the relationship between climate and forest health, I conducted a correlation analysis. This involved calculating Pearson correlation coefficients to determine the strength and direction of the relationship between temperature, rainfall, and dieback. Here’s what the correlation analysis revealed:

  • A weak positive relationship between temperature and forest dieback (0.023). This suggests that while temperature has a slight influence, its effect on forest dieback is minimal.
  • A weak negative relationship between rainfall and dieback (-0.29). This indicates that as rainfall decreases, dieback tends to increase, particularly during drought periods.

While neither correlation was strong, the findings pointed to rainfall variability as a more critical factor in influencing dieback.

(06) Building a Predictive Model.

The final step was to develop a predictive model capable of estimating future forest dieback based on the climate data. I experimented with several machine learning algorithms, including:

  • Support Vector Regression (SVR)
  • Random Forest
  • Gradient Boosting
  • Neural Networks

After testing each model, the Random Forest model emerged as the best performer, achieving a Mean Absolute Error (MAE) of 257.45 on the test data. This model identified rainfall as the most important factor in predicting dieback, with temperature lag (the effect of past temperature) also playing a key role.

Results: What Did We Learn?

(01) Rainfall Drives Forest Health.

The most important finding from the study is the critical role that rainfall plays in determining forest dieback in Horton Plains. While temperature changes were observed, the weak correlation between temperature and dieback suggests that rainfall variability, particularly drought conditions, is a key driver of forest health decline.

(02) Droughts Lead to Increased Dieback.

The trend analysis showed that dieback levels spiked during dry years, confirming that drought conditions are a significant factor in forest degradation. These findings suggest that as climate change continues to alter rainfall patterns, Horton Plains’ forests could face increasing challenges in the future.

(03) Predictive Model Provides Conservation Tool.

The Random Forest model offers a practical tool for forecasting forest dieback based on expected climate conditions. This model can help conservationists and forest managers anticipate dieback events and take preemptive action, such as implementing water management strategies during dry periods.

Challenges and Limitations :

While the research provided valuable insights, it also faced some limitations:

To understand the changes occurring in Horton Plains, I focused on two main types of data: monthly climate data and information on forest dieback.

  • Lack of monthly dieback data: Due to the availability of only annual satellite images, I had to estimate dieback on a yearly basis, which may not fully capture short-term changes in forest health.
  • Weak correlations: The correlation between temperature and dieback was weak, suggesting that other factors (such as soil conditions, pests, or human activities) likely play a role in forest health.
  • Model complexity: While the Random Forest model performed well, it does not account for all potential ecological factors influencing dieback. Future models should aim to include more environmental variables for better accuracy.

What’s Next? Future Research Directions

There are several areas for future research that could build on these findings:

  • 1. Incorporating additional environmental factors: Adding variables such as soil moisture, pest outbreaks, and human activities could improve the predictive power of the model.
  • 2. Using higher-resolution satellite data: Access to monthly satellite images could help provide a more detailed view of how forest dieback responds to short-term climate fluctuations.
  • 3. Exploring non-linear relationships: The weak linear correlations suggest that more complex, non-linear relationships may exist between climate and dieback. Future research should explore non-linear modeling techniques to capture these dynamics.

Conclusion: Implications for Conservation

In summary, this research underscores the importance of rainfall variability in maintaining the health of Horton Plains’ forests. As climate change continues to alter weather patterns, particularly increasing the risk of droughts, proactive measures will be essential to safeguard these vital ecosystems. The predictive model developed in this study offers a useful tool for anticipating forest dieback events and guiding conservation efforts in Horton Plains and other tropical regions facing similar challenges. By continuing to explore the complex interactions between climate and forest health, we can better understand how to protect these critical ecosystems for future generations.
